Material Transparency

How Auralis Describes Crystal Materials and Natural Variation

Auralis uses a disclosure-first approach: describe what is known, state what is uncertain, explain relevant treatments or care needs, and avoid unsupported certification claims.

Quick answer

Auralis should not describe every bead simply as “100% natural” without supporting information. The product description should identify the known material direction, disclose known or suspected treatments and clearly distinguish natural, laboratory-grown, imitation and uncertain materials.

Material Terms

What common descriptions mean

These terms are not interchangeable. The wording used for a quotation should match the information available for the specific material.

Natural

Natural mineral material

Formed in nature, then cut, polished and drilled for jewelry. Natural origin does not automatically mean untreated or individually laboratory tested.

Treated

Treated material

A natural or other gem material whose color, clarity or appearance has been altered by a process such as heating, dyeing, coating, filling, irradiation or impregnation.

Laboratory-Grown

Synthetic material

Produced in a laboratory and sharing key chemical, optical and physical characteristics with a natural counterpart. Its laboratory origin must be made clear.

Imitation

Simulant or look-alike

A different material chosen to resemble another gem. It may be glass, resin, another mineral or a composite and should not be presented as the gem it imitates.

Composite

Assembled or composite material

Made from two or more joined components. The description should not imply that the complete item is a single untreated natural stone.

Unverified

Uncertain material information

When origin or treatment cannot be confirmed, the uncertainty should be stated. Auralis should not upgrade an uncertain description into a definite natural or untreated claim.

Auralis Disclosure Process

What should be confirmed before quote and payment

1

Identify the known material direction

Confirm the trade name, color direction, bead size, metal parts and available supplier information for the proposed design.

2

Disclose known or suspected treatments

State treatments or uncertainty when they may affect appearance, durability, care or value. Do not describe a material as untreated when that has not been verified.

3

Show expected natural variation

Explain that color, transparency, texture, pattern and inclusions can differ from photographs and from bead to bead.

4

Confirm specifications and care

Confirm nominal bead size, bracelet size, metal components and any material-specific care note before the order proceeds.

5

Use laboratory claims only when specific

Do not use “GIA-certified,” “lab certified” or similar wording unless a valid report applies to that specific stone or item and can be reviewed.

6

Quote independent testing separately

When testing is requested, feasibility, laboratory choice, cost, timing and the exact item to be tested must be confirmed separately.

Independent References

Why treatment and origin disclosures matter

The Gemological Institute of America explains that treatments can change color, clarity, durability and care requirements, and that laboratory-grown material must be identified clearly. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission’s Jewelry Guides emphasize truthful representation of material type, treatment, quality and other characteristics. Auralis is not affiliated with or endorsed by GIA or the FTC. References: GIA: An Introduction to Gem Treatments · GIA: Synthetic Gem Materials · FTC: Advertising Gemstones.

FAQ

Material authenticity questions

Does natural crystal always look identical to the product photo?

No. Natural mineral materials can vary in color, transparency, pattern and inclusions. Lighting, camera settings and screens can also change how colors appear. The exact material direction should be confirmed before production or shipment.

Does Auralis call every stone GIA-certified?

No. Auralis should only use a laboratory or certification claim when a valid report applies to the specific item. A product description is not a substitute for independent gemological testing.

What if a material may be dyed, coated, heated or otherwise treated?

Known or reasonably suspected treatments should be disclosed before the order is confirmed, especially when they affect appearance, care or value. If information is uncertain, the uncertainty should be stated rather than presenting the material as untreated.

Can I ask for independent testing?

You may ask before ordering. Feasibility, laboratory choice, cost, timing and whether testing is appropriate for the specific material must be confirmed separately before payment.

Are bead sizes exact?

Bracelet bead sizes are normally described by nominal diameter. Small variation can occur because of polishing, drilling and natural material differences. Final specifications should be confirmed with the quote.

How should I care for a crystal bracelet?

Avoid strong impact, prolonged chemical exposure and unnecessary moisture. Material-specific care may differ, particularly for dyed, coated, filled, porous or softer materials, so follow any care note supplied with the design.
